# Week 13 — BLE 5 and BLE Mesh on the Pico W

> *Bluetooth Low Energy is the radio that won. Not because it is fast — it tops out around 1.4 Mbit/s of application throughput on a good day with a 2M PHY and a coalesced connection, an order of magnitude below Wi-Fi — but because it is the radio that every phone in every pocket already speaks, it costs a few cents of silicon and a few microamps of average current, and its protocol stack, after a decade of revisions, finally documents every byte that crosses the air. This week you stop treating BLE as a black box behind a phone app and start reading the link-layer packets: the 37-bit access address, the 39 channels, the connection-interval negotiation, the GATT attribute table that is really just a flat array of (handle, type, value) tuples. The Pico W carries an Infineon CYW43439 that does Wi-Fi and Bluetooth on the same die; the Pico SDK drives its BLE controller through BTstack, an open-source, MIT-licensed host stack that exposes every layer from HCI up to GATT and BLE Mesh. You will write a GATT server, a GATT client, an advertiser, a scanner, a bonded secure connection, and — by Friday — a three-node BLE Mesh network that relays a sensor reading across a hop the source node cannot reach directly.*

Welcome to Week 13 of C7, the first week of Phase III (IoT Fleet & Edge). Last week you took the FreeRTOS sensor hub and re-implemented it on Zephyr with `west`, an overlay device tree, and the Zephyr shell, then diffed the binary sizes and learned that the vendor-neutral RTOS costs you ~40 KB of flash and buys you 80% of the board-support package for free. This week you keep the same Pico W on the bench but turn on its radio. The mental model shifts from "one MCU, many peripherals on a wire" to "one MCU, one antenna, many devices in the air sharing 2.4 GHz with every other Bluetooth, Wi-Fi, Zigbee, and microwave oven within forty metres." The wire is gone; the discipline of bounded latency and clean state machines stays.

Bluetooth Low Energy is specified in the *Bluetooth Core Specification* — the current published revision is 5.4 (Bluetooth SIG, 2023-01-31), a single 3,256-page PDF, free at <https://www.bluetooth.com/specifications/specs/core-specification-5-4/>. You will not read all of it; nobody has. This week we read four pieces of it: Vol 6 Part B (the Link Layer, the bytes on the air), Vol 3 Part C (the Generic Access Profile, GAP — how devices discover and connect to each other), Vol 3 Part G (the Generic Attribute Profile, GATT — how devices expose and exchange structured data), and Vol 3 Part H (the Security Manager, pairing and bonding). BLE Mesh is a *separate* specification — the *Bluetooth Mesh Protocol* 1.1 (Bluetooth SIG, 2023-09-12, free at <https://www.bluetooth.com/specifications/specs/mesh-protocol-1-1/>) — that runs *on top of* the BLE advertising channels but defines its own provisioning, addressing, and model layers. We treat Mesh as its own topic in Lecture 3.

The Pico W's radio is not on the RP2040. The RP2040 has no radio of any kind. The Pico W adds an Infineon (formerly Cypress) CYW43439 — a combo chip with a 2.4 GHz Wi-Fi 4 radio and a Bluetooth 5.2 controller — connected to the RP2040 over a 4-pin SPI-like bus that the SDK's `pico_cyw43_arch` library drives. For Bluetooth specifically, the CYW43439 implements the *Controller* (the lower half of the BLE stack: Link Layer, PHY, the part that owns the antenna and the precise timing) and exposes it over HCI (the Host-Controller Interface, Core Spec Vol 4 Part E). The *Host* (L2CAP, ATT, GATT, GAP, SMP) runs in software on the RP2040, and the SDK ships **BTstack** (BlueKitchen GmbH, dual-licensed: free for non-commercial and open-source use, commercial license otherwise; source at <https://github.com/bluekitchen/btstack>) to provide it. When you call `att_server_init()` or `gap_advertisements_enable(1)` this week, you are calling BTstack, which marshals HCI commands down the cyw43 SPI link to the controller. This split — host in C on the MCU, controller on the combo chip — is the same architecture as a Linux box driving a USB Bluetooth dongle through BlueZ; the only difference is that the "dongle" is soldered to the board and the link is SPI, not USB.

The mini-project is a **BLE Mesh occupancy fleet**: three Pico W nodes form a managed-flooding mesh, one node hosts a PIR occupancy sensor and publishes a Generic OnOff state when motion is detected, the other two subscribe to that state and drive an LED, and — critically — the publisher and one subscriber are placed out of direct radio range of each other, so the third node must act as a *relay* for the message to arrive. You provision all three nodes with a small host-side Python tool (`cc-mesh-provision.py`) that walks the PB-ADV provisioning bearer, runs the ECDH key exchange, and hands each node its unicast address and network key. On the bench you then walk out of the room, the PIR fires, and the LED two hops away lights up. The whole thing is BLE Mesh 1.1 §3 (the mesh networking layers) and §5 (the foundation and Generic models), implemented on BTstack's mesh stack, and it is the load-bearing artifact of the week.

A note on the syllabus: the C7 syllabus lists this material as the Week 14 slot ("BLE 5 and BLE Mesh") and references the Nordic nRF52840 and the Zephyr BLE stack as one possible platform. We deliver it here as Week 13 on the Pico W with BTstack to keep the hardware identical to Weeks 1–12 — you already own a Pico W, you already have the SDK built, and you do not need to learn `west` and the Nordic SoftDevice in the same week you are learning GATT. The concepts (GAP, GATT, SMP, Mesh provisioning, models, relay) are stack-independent; BTstack's API names differ from Zephyr's and Nordic's, but the Core Spec section numbers are the same on every platform. Where the nRF52840 or Zephyr does something materially differently, the lecture notes say so.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Explain** the BLE protocol stack layer by layer, and locate each layer in the Core Spec. From the bottom: the **PHY** (Vol 6 Part A) defines the GFSK modulation, the 40 RF channels at 2 MHz spacing from 2402 to 2480 MHz, and the four PHY variants (LE 1M, LE 2M, LE Coded S=2, LE Coded S=8). The **Link Layer** (Vol 6 Part B) defines advertising and data channel packets, the 32-bit access address, the connection state machine, and the channel-hopping algorithm. **L2CAP** (Vol 3 Part A) fragments and reassembles upper-layer PDUs over the link. **ATT** (Vol 3 Part F) is the wire protocol for reading and writing attributes. **GATT** (Vol 3 Part G) is the profile built on ATT that defines services and characteristics. **GAP** (Vol 3 Part C) defines roles (broadcaster, observer, peripheral, central) and the discovery and connection procedures. **SMP** (Vol 3 Part H) is the Security Manager that does pairing and key distribution. You will draw this stack on a whiteboard and place a packet capture's bytes against the right layer.
- **Distinguish** advertising from connections. A BLE device that only *advertises* is a **broadcaster**; a device that only *listens* to advertisements is an **observer**; this is connectionless, one-way, and the basis of beacons (iBeacon, Eddystone) and of BLE Mesh. A device that accepts a connection is a **peripheral**; the device that initiates is a **central**. Advertising happens on the three *primary advertising channels* (37, 38, 39 — chosen to dodge the common Wi-Fi channels 1, 6, 11), at an interval you choose between 20 ms and 10.24 s (Core Spec Vol 6 Part B §4.4.2.2). A connection, once formed, hops across the 37 *data channels* on a schedule both sides compute from the channel map and hop increment exchanged in the `CONNECT_IND` PDU. You will implement both an advertiser (Exercise 1) and a connectable GATT server (the mini-project).
- **Build** a GATT server: services, characteristics, descriptors, and the attribute table. GATT models data as a flat **attribute table** — an array of `(handle, type/UUID, permissions, value)` tuples (Core Spec Vol 3 Part F §3.1, the ATT attribute definition). A **service** is a grouping declaration (UUID `0x2800` for a primary service); a **characteristic** is a value plus a declaration (UUID `0x2803`) that carries the properties byte (Read/Write/Notify/Indicate) and the handle of the value; a **descriptor** like the Client Characteristic Configuration Descriptor (CCCD, UUID `0x2902`) lets a client subscribe to notifications. BTstack generates the attribute table from a `.gatt` text file at build time with its `compile_gatt.py` script; you write the `.gatt`, the script emits a `profile_data` C array, and `att_server_init()` serves it. You build a custom service with three characteristics in the mini-project.
- **Implement** a GATT client. The mirror image: connect to a peripheral, run *service discovery* (the ATT `Read By Group Type Request` for primary services), then *characteristic discovery* (`Read By Type Request`), then read/write the value handle or subscribe to notifications by writing `0x0001` to the CCCD. BTstack's `gatt_client` API drives this as an asynchronous event-callback sequence. Exercise 2 is a GATT client that discovers and reads the Battery Service (`0x180F`) Battery Level characteristic (`0x2A19`) from any nearby device.
- **Reason** about BLE security: pairing, bonding, and the LE Secure Connections handshake. **Pairing** is the act of establishing keys for one session; **bonding** is storing those keys so the next connection skips pairing. **LE Legacy pairing** (Bluetooth 4.0–4.1) used a weak key-agreement that is trivially broken by a passive sniffer (the `crackle` tool does it in milliseconds). **LE Secure Connections** (Bluetooth 4.2+, Core Spec Vol 3 Part H §2.3.5.6) uses ECDH on the NIST P-256 curve to derive a shared secret no sniffer can recover, then one of four *association models* — Just Works, Passkey Entry, Numeric Comparison, Out Of Band — to authenticate against a man-in-the-middle, depending on each device's I/O capabilities. You will bond a Pico W to your phone with Numeric Comparison in the mini-project and watch `crackle` fail to recover the keys.
- **Use** the BLE 5 features that matter for embedded fleets. **LE 2M PHY** (Bluetooth 5.0) doubles the symbol rate to 2 Msym/s, halving on-air time and roughly halving the energy per packet — at the cost of ~3 dB of receiver sensitivity (shorter range). **LE Coded PHY** (Long Range, S=2 and S=8 coding) trades data rate for ~2–4× the range by adding forward error correction; the S=8 variant runs at 125 kbit/s and can reach hundreds of metres line-of-sight. **Extended Advertising** (Bluetooth 5.0, Core Spec Vol 6 Part B §4.4.2) moves the advertising payload off the three primary channels onto the 37 data channels via a pointer (`AUX_ADV_IND`), raising the maximum advertising payload from 31 bytes to 1650 bytes and enabling *periodic advertising* (deterministic, connectionless broadcast that a scanner can sync to). The CYW43439 controller supports 2M and Extended Advertising; LE Coded PHY support depends on the controller firmware revision — we test for it at runtime and the lecture explains the check.
- **Explain** BLE Mesh as a flooding network, not a connection network. BLE Mesh (Mesh Protocol 1.1) does **not** use GATT connections between mesh nodes. It uses *managed flooding*: every message is broadcast as a non-connectable advertisement on the three advertising channels; every node that hears it and is configured as a **relay** re-broadcasts it (decrementing a TTL to bound the flood); the network layer deduplicates with a sequence-number cache. There is no routing table, no parent/child hierarchy, no master. This is robust (any node can relay, so the network self-heals around dead nodes) and wasteful (a single message floods the whole network). Mesh addressing has three kinds: **unicast** (one element), **group** (a multicast subscription, e.g. "all lights in the kitchen"), and **virtual** (a 128-bit label hashed into a 16-bit address). You will build a 3-node flood network in the mini-project.
- **Walk** BLE Mesh provisioning. An unprovisioned node is useless — it has no keys and no address. **Provisioning** (Mesh Protocol 1.1 §6) is the secure onboarding ceremony: the provisioner (your phone, or our Python tool) and the new node exchange ECDH public keys over the unprovisioned *provisioning bearer* (PB-ADV over advertisements, or PB-GATT over a GATT connection), derive a shared secret, authenticate with an OOB value (a number shown on the device or printed on a label), and then the provisioner sends the node its **unicast address**, the **network key (NetKey)**, and an **IV index**. After provisioning, an *application* assigns **application keys (AppKeys)** and **publish/subscribe addresses** to the node's models via the Configuration Client. You will provision three nodes with `cc-mesh-provision.py`.
- **Define** mesh **models**. A model is the BLE Mesh equivalent of a GATT service: a standardized set of states, messages, and behaviors. The **Configuration Server** model (mandatory on every node) lets a provisioner configure the node post-provisioning. The **Generic OnOff Server/Client** models carry a boolean (light on/off, occupancy detected/clear). The **Sensor Server** model carries typed sensor readings. Models live in **elements** (a node has one or more elements, each with its own unicast address); a light fixture with two independently-controllable bulbs is one node with two elements. Your occupancy node implements a Generic OnOff Server (publishing) and the subscriber nodes implement Generic OnOff Clients (subscribing to the same group address).
- **Configure** a relay and reason about TTL. Each mesh message carries a **TTL** (time-to-live, 0–127). The originating node sets it (default 7 in most stacks); each relay decrements it by one before re-broadcasting; a message with TTL 0 or 1 is not relayed. TTL bounds the flood — without it, a message would circulate forever. In the mini-project, the publisher is two hops from one subscriber, so the message must be relayed exactly once (TTL goes 7 → 6 at the relay), and you confirm with a sniffer that the relay node re-broadcast the access message. You will deliberately set the publisher's TTL to 1, watch the far subscriber go dark, and explain why.
- **Bridge** BLE Mesh to MQTT (concept; the full bridge is a homework problem). A real occupancy fleet does not stop at the mesh — it forwards the occupancy state to a building-management system. A *gateway* node subscribes to the mesh group address, and on the Pico W's other radio (Wi-Fi, which you brought up two phases ago) publishes the state to an MQTT topic. The mini-project's gateway hook is stubbed and the full Wi-Fi-side bridge is homework problem 5; we keep the mini-project's core on BLE so the radio stays the focus.
- **Capture** BLE over the air with free tools. You will use the **nRF Sniffer for Bluetooth LE** (Nordic, free firmware for a spare nRF52840 dongle or a Pico-based sniffer build, decodes into Wireshark) or, if you have neither, the **Android nRF Connect** app's log and the **`btmon`** tool on a Linux host with a Bluetooth adapter. A sniffer is to BLE what a logic analyzer was to SPI in Week 8 — the difference between guessing and knowing. Every exercise this week has a "capture it" step.

## Topics covered

- **The BLE stack, layer by layer.** PHY → Link Layer → L2CAP → ATT → GATT, with GAP and SMP off to the side. The Controller (CYW43439, owns PHY + Link Layer) vs the Host (BTstack on the RP2040, owns L2CAP up through GAP/GATT/SMP), connected by HCI. Cite Core Spec Vol 1 Part A §1 (architecture overview) and Vol 4 Part E (HCI). The split matters because the timing-critical parts (the 150 µs inter-frame space, the connection-event scheduling) live in the controller's firmware where the RP2040's jitter cannot reach them.
- **The 40 channels and the three PHYs.** 40 RF channels at 2 MHz spacing, 2402–2480 MHz. Channels 37, 38, 39 are the primary advertising channels (placed at 2402, 2426, 2480 MHz to sit in the gaps between Wi-Fi channels 1/6/11). Channels 0–36 are data channels. PHYs: LE 1M (1 Msym/s, the original), LE 2M (2 Msym/s, Bluetooth 5.0, half the air-time), LE Coded S=2 (500 kbit/s, ~2× range) and S=8 (125 kbit/s, ~4× range). Cite Vol 6 Part A §2 and §3.
- **Advertising: packets, intervals, types.** The advertising PDU types: `ADV_IND` (connectable, scannable, undirected), `ADV_DIRECT_IND` (connectable, directed at one peer), `ADV_NONCONN_IND` (non-connectable — beacons, mesh), `ADV_SCAN_IND` (scannable non-connectable), and `SCAN_RSP` (the response to an active scanner's `SCAN_REQ`). The 31-byte legacy advertising payload and its AD-structure TLV format (length, AD type, data). The advertising interval and the random ~0–10 ms delay added each event to avoid persistent collisions. Cite Vol 6 Part B §2.3 (advertising PDUs) and §4.4.2 (advertising).
- **Connections: the CONNECT_IND, intervals, and supervision timeout.** When a central wants to connect, it sends `CONNECT_IND` carrying the access address, CRC init, window size/offset, connection interval (7.5 ms–4 s), peripheral latency (how many events the peripheral may skip), supervision timeout (how long without a packet before the link is declared dead), the channel map, and the hop increment. Both sides then compute the channel-hopping sequence independently. The connection-interval/latency/timeout triple is the central knob for the power-vs-latency trade-off. Cite Vol 6 Part B §4.5.
- **GATT: services, characteristics, descriptors, the attribute table.** The attribute is `(handle, UUID, permissions, value)`. Services group characteristics; characteristics group a value + properties + descriptors. Standard UUIDs are 16-bit (assigned by the SIG: `0x180F` Battery Service, `0x2A19` Battery Level, `0x2902` CCCD); custom services use 128-bit UUIDs. The properties byte: Broadcast, Read, WriteWithoutResponse, Write, Notify, Indicate, AuthenticatedSignedWrites, ExtendedProperties. Notify vs Indicate: Notify is fire-and-forget (no ATT-layer ack), Indicate is acknowledged (the client must send a Confirmation). Cite Vol 3 Part G §3 (service and characteristic definitions) and Part F §3 (the attribute and ATT PDUs).
- **The ATT protocol on the wire.** The request/response PDUs: `Exchange MTU`, `Find Information`, `Read By Type` (used for characteristic discovery), `Read By Group Type` (primary service discovery), `Read`, `Read Blob`, `Write`, `Write Command`, `Handle Value Notification`, `Handle Value Indication`/`Confirmation`. The default ATT MTU is 23 bytes (so a single un-fragmented notification carries 20 bytes of value: 23 − 1 opcode − 2 handle); the `Exchange MTU` negotiates up to 517. Cite Vol 3 Part F §3.4.
- **Security: pairing, bonding, the SMP handshake.** Pairing phases: Phase 1 (feature exchange — I/O capabilities, OOB flag, bonding flag, key-distribution flags), Phase 2 (key agreement — Legacy STK or Secure Connections LTK via ECDH P-256), Phase 3 (transport-specific key distribution — IRK for privacy, CSRK for signed writes). The four association models and how the I/O-capability matrix (Vol 3 Part H §2.3.5.1, Table 2.8) selects between Just Works, Passkey Entry, Numeric Comparison, and OOB. Bonding stores the LTK (and IRK/CSRK) keyed by the peer's identity address so the next connection encrypts immediately. Cite Vol 3 Part H §2.3 and §2.4.
- **BLE 5: 2M PHY, Coded PHY, Extended and Periodic Advertising.** The `LE Set PHY` HCI command and the `LE_PHY_UPDATE_COMPLETE` event. The trade: 2M halves air-time and energy, costs ~3 dB sensitivity; Coded S=8 quadruples range, costs 8× air-time. Extended Advertising's `ADV_EXT_IND` on a primary channel pointing to `AUX_ADV_IND` on a secondary (data) channel, raising the payload to 1650 bytes and adding periodic advertising trains a scanner can lock onto. Cite Vol 6 Part B §2.3.4 (extended advertising PDUs) and §4.4.2.4 (periodic advertising). Note which features the CYW43439 controller exposes and how to query them via `LE Read Local Supported Features`.
- **BLE Mesh: managed flooding, layers, addressing.** Mesh sits on `ADV_NONCONN_IND` advertisements (the Mesh "advertising bearer") — it is connectionless. The layer stack: Model → Foundation Model → Access → Upper Transport (application-layer encryption with the AppKey, plus a 32/64-bit MIC) → Lower Transport (segmentation/reassembly) → Network (network-layer encryption with the NetKey, the TTL, the sequence number, obfuscation of the header) → Bearer (advertising or GATT). Three address types: unicast, group (`0xC000`–`0xFEFF`), virtual (label-UUID-derived). Managed flooding with TTL-bounded relay and a sequence-number replay/dedup cache. Cite Mesh Protocol 1.1 §3 (network architecture) and §3.4 (addresses).
- **Mesh provisioning.** The PB-ADV bearer; the five provisioning PDUs phases (Invite, Capabilities, Start, Public Key exchange, Confirmation/Random, Data). ECDH on P-256 to derive the shared secret; the OOB authentication value; the encrypted Provisioning Data PDU that delivers the unicast address, NetKey, key index, IV index, and flags. After provisioning, the device is a node but its models are unconfigured; the Configuration Client then sets AppKeys, publish addresses, subscribe addresses, and the relay/proxy/friend/low-power feature flags. Cite Mesh Protocol 1.1 §6 (provisioning).
- **Mesh models.** Configuration Server (mandatory), Health Server (mandatory), and the Generic, Sensor, and Lighting model families. The Generic OnOff Server/Client pair we use for occupancy. Publish (a model sends its state to a configured address on a schedule or on change) vs Subscribe (a model accepts messages sent to a group address it is subscribed to). Elements as the unit of addressing. Cite Mesh Model spec (Bluetooth Mesh Model 1.1) §3 (Generic models) and the Mesh Protocol §5 (foundation models).
- **Relay and TTL.** The relay feature flag; the relay-retransmit count and interval; how TTL bounds the flood; why a network with no relays is a star and a network with all-relays is a full flood; the heartbeat mechanism that lets the network estimate hop counts. The replay-protection list and the sequence-number-and-IV-index pair that prevents an attacker from replaying a captured "turn on" message. Cite Mesh Protocol 1.1 §3.6 (relay) and §3.8 (replay protection).
- **Sniffing and debugging.** The nRF Sniffer → Wireshark pipeline; what a BLE Wireshark capture shows (advertising PDUs with the full AD structure decoded, connection events, ATT requests/responses with handles and values, encrypted vs cleartext); how to follow a connection (the sniffer must catch the `CONNECT_IND` to compute the hop sequence and follow the connection); why an encrypted/bonded link shows opaque ATT payloads unless you give Wireshark the LTK. Cite the nRF Sniffer user guide. This is the week's logic analyzer.

## What is intentionally out of scope this week

- **Bluetooth Classic (BR/EDR).** The Pico W's controller is BLE-only for the SDK's purposes; Classic profiles (A2DP audio, RFCOMM serial, HFP) are a separate stack and a separate world. Everything this week is Low Energy. If you need to stream audio over Bluetooth, that is Classic A2DP or LE Audio (a 5.2+ feature with its own isochronous channels), and neither is on the menu here.
- **LE Audio and Isochronous Channels.** Bluetooth 5.2 added LC3 codec, isochronous channels (CIS/BIS), and Auracast broadcast audio. The CYW43439 is a 5.2 controller and *can* do isochronous channels in principle, but BTstack's LE Audio support is incomplete and the topic deserves its own week. We note it exists and move on.
- **Production mesh security: key refresh, IV update, node removal/blacklisting.** A real mesh deployment rotates the NetKey periodically (Key Refresh Procedure, Mesh Protocol §3.10.4) and increments the IV index to keep the sequence-number space from exhausting (IV Update Procedure, §3.10.5), and securely removes a compromised node from the network. We provision and run a static mesh; key refresh and IV update are mentioned in Lecture 3 and left for a production course.
- **The Bluetooth privacy feature in depth (RPA rotation).** Resolvable Private Addresses (Vol 6 Part B §1.3.2.2) let a device rotate its advertised address every ~15 minutes so trackers cannot follow it, with the bonded peer resolving the real identity via the IRK. We enable privacy in the bonding exercise and explain the IRK, but the full RPA-resolution dance and its corner cases (the "address-resolution-offload" controller feature) are out of scope.
- **Matter and Thread.** Matter-over-Thread is next week (Week 14 in this delivery: LoRaWAN, Zigbee, Thread, Matter). BLE shows up there only as the *commissioning* bearer (a new Matter device advertises over BLE so your phone can hand it the Thread network credentials). We build the BLE half here; the Thread half is next week.

## A note on debugging BLE blind

You will be tempted to debug this week with `printf` over the USB CDC port and nothing else. It will not be enough. BLE is a timing-and-bytes protocol where the interesting failures — a malformed AD structure that makes your advertiser invisible, a CCCD you forgot to write so notifications never arrive, a mesh message that floods but never relays because you left the relay flag off — are *on the air*, not in your code's control flow. The single highest-leverage purchase of this week is a BLE sniffer (a $10 nRF52840 dongle with the nRF Sniffer firmware), and the single highest-leverage habit is to capture every exercise. When your advertiser "does not work," the sniffer tells you in five seconds whether the advertisements are on the air and malformed, or not on the air at all — two completely different bugs that look identical from the device side.

The Wi-Fi half of the Pico W's radio you brought up in an earlier phase; you know `printf`-style debugging on this board already. BLE is different in kind: half the state machine lives in the CYW43439 controller's firmware, which you cannot single-step. The sniffer is your window into that half. Treat it the way you treated the Saleae in Week 7 and the SWO trace in Week 9 — not optional, foundational.
